UX lead on a 3-year engagement at PostNord — app redesign, continuous optimisation, and team leadership across one of Scandinavia's most complex logistics products.

Overview

The work spanned a major redesign initiative followed by two years of continuous iteration — moving between strategic UX, detailed flow design, usability testing, A/B experimentation, and cross-functional team leadership. The scope touched the product's most critical surfaces: delivery tracking, account management, and the communication and identity flows that underpin the entire logistics experience.

The real challenge was not simply to redesign screens. It was to make a technically constrained and operationally complex system feel simple and predictable for users.

The Challenge

PostNord's app operated in an environment shaped by fragmented backend systems, regulatory requirements, and an enormous range of delivery edge cases. Users expected the product to be clear and reliable regardless of what was happening behind the scenes.

System complexity

  • Multiple delivery flows and edge cases
  • Fragmented backend systems and technical constraints
  • Communication logic across push, SMS, and paper notifications
  • VAT, customs, and regulatory requirements
  • Migration from legacy to new app experience

User expectations

  • High demand for transparency and reliability
  • Sensitive account data — addresses, phone numbers, identity
  • Clear next steps at every stage of delivery
  • Consistent experience across versions and flows

Simplifying the tracking experience

Tracking was the most important — and inherently most complex — part of the product. Users needed to understand what was happening with their delivery and what, if anything, they needed to do next. Even when the underlying systems were inconsistent, the experience needed to feel structured and trustworthy.

The focus was on reducing cognitive load in delivery-related choices, clarifying statuses and next steps, and making flows feel coherent even when edge cases created ambiguity underneath.

Redesigned tracking experience — statuses, next steps, and delivery choices

Turning complex systems into clear UX

A significant part of the work involved translating operational and regulatory complexity into flows that felt manageable for users. This meant working closely with domain experts to understand processes that most designers rarely encounter — VAT and customs handling, BankID-based identity verification, address ownership across multiple delivery contexts, and multi-channel notification orchestration.

Each of these required detailed system mapping before any design work could begin, followed by careful information architecture and interaction design for the many edge cases involved.

Account settings and customs flow mapping

Outcome

Over three years the product evolved through both a major redesign and sustained optimisation. Tracking became clearer and more usable. Account and identity-related flows were simplified. Technically complex journeys — customs, VAT, migration — were given coherent structure for the first time. Weekly usability testing became a core part of the product process, and collaboration between UX, engineering, and data grew significantly stronger.

Most importantly, the work treated launch as a starting point rather than a finish line — building a continuous improvement practice that outlasted the redesign itself.

Key Learnings

On constraints

Great UX is often about making constraints invisible to users. Designing within technical realities — rather than around them — produces more durable outcomes than ideal flows that can't be built.

On complexity

Complex systems don't need more features — they need more structure. Continuous testing creates the confidence to make that simplification without guessing.
