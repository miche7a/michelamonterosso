PostNord is Scandinavia's leading postal and logistics provider. Over three years I worked on their consumer app through continuous UX challenges and improvements, team lead work and tight collaboration with a team of 30 people.

Three years on the PostNord app: continuous improvement, then redesign: Making a technically complex logistics product feel simple and reliable over time.

The Challenge

PostNord's app operates in a very complex environment where users expect clarity and reliability regardless of what is happening behind the scenes. Handling backend limitations, legacy issues, multiple stakeholders and needs in different markets was part of my daily work. But with millions of users relying on the service, I had to make sure all steps of the UX felt smooth and understandable.

Main hurdles across the system

  • Creation of multiple delivery flows, discovering and highlighting dependencies
  • Work around fragmented backend systems and technical constraints
  • Tie together the communication logic across several channels such as push, SMS, and paper notifications
  • Map out VAT, customs, and identity-verification flows
  • Migration from legacy to new app experience

Approach

Several principles shaped the work across all three years:

Building understanding before redesigning

Having worked on the app for two years before the redesign, I entered that phase with a clear picture of recurring pain points, technical constraints, and which areas needed structural change versus iteration. The redesign was grounded in long-term product knowledge and close collaboration with stakeholders.

Continuous user-centred iteration

Weekly usability testing ran throughout — validating ideas early, identifying friction in both large and small changes, and feeding real user insight into both live improvements and redesign decisions. This created a tight loop between design work and actual user behaviour.

Weekly usability testing and system mapping ran throughout the engagement

Designing within constraints

Close collaboration with engineering was essential from the start. Rather than designing idealised flows, the focus was on deeply understanding backend limitations and shaping solutions that were feasible and stable.

Simplifying the tracking experience

Tracking was the most important and inherently complex part of the app. Users needed to understand what was happening with their delivery and what, if anything, they needed to do next (even when the underlying systems were unable to give feedback).

The focus was on reducing cognitive load in delivery-related choices, clarifying statuses and next steps, and improving the logic of key journeys.

Redesigned tracking experience — statuses, next steps, and delivery choices

Turning complex systems into clear UX

A significant part of the work involved translating operational and regulatory complexity into experiences that felt manageable. This meant working closely with domain experts to understand processes most designers rarely encounter: VAT and customs handling, BankID-based identity verification, address ownership, and multi-channel notification logic.

Each area required detailed system mapping before any design work could begin, followed by careful information architecture and interaction design across the many edge cases involved.

Account and identity flows, VAT and customs process mapping

Outcome

After years of design evolution, the team came to a point of no return: the system was not scalable anymore, requests and new features made it so that screens became overwhelming. We were not able to allocate the right information in the right place. Our team proposed the redesign to PostNord, and I lead the design planning across several months. I was respinsible to frame the overall UX experience with simplicity and scalability in mind, solving user and business problems I had been encountering for a long time.

The redesign was not an isolated event. It grew out of sustained product work, which helped create a more durable direction. The project has been handed over to PostNord's in-house team.
