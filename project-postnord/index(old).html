Three years on the PostNord app — continuous improvement, then redesign — making a technically complex logistics product feel simple and reliable over time.

Overview

For the first two years I focused on maintaining and improving the live product — continuous UX work, weekly usability testing, and iterative enhancements. During the final year I worked on a larger redesign initiative while still supporting the live app. I was not brought in only for the redesign. By the time it began, I had already built a deep understanding of the product, the users, and the operational complexity behind it.

PostNord had worked with our agency for around ten years. During this period the setup involved about 30 people across three teams — I worked within one of them. Toward the end of the engagement, PostNord established an in-house team to continue the work.

The Challenge

PostNord's app operated in a highly complex environment where users expected clarity and reliability regardless of what was happening behind the scenes. The challenge was not just to redesign — it was to improve the live product meaningfully while also shaping a better long-term experience.

System complexity

  • Multiple delivery flows and dependencies
  • Fragmented backend systems and technical constraints
  • Communication logic across push, SMS, and paper notifications
  • VAT, customs, and identity-related flows
  • Migration from legacy to new app experience

The dual challenge

  • Improve the current experience continuously
  • Define a better long-term direction in parallel
  • Maintain reliability and trust throughout
  • Make a constrained system feel simple and predictable

Approach

Several principles shaped the work across all three years.

Building understanding before redesigning

Having worked on the app for two years before the redesign, I entered that phase with a clear picture of recurring pain points, technical constraints, and which areas needed structural change versus iteration. The redesign was grounded in long-term product knowledge, not a short discovery window.

Continuous user-centred iteration

Weekly usability testing ran throughout — validating ideas early, identifying friction in both large and small changes, and feeding real user insight into both live improvements and redesign decisions. This created a tight loop between design work and actual user behaviour.

Weekly usability testing and system mapping ran throughout the engagement

Designing within constraints

Close collaboration with engineering was essential from the start. Rather than designing idealised flows, the focus was on deeply understanding backend limitations and shaping solutions that were feasible and stable — making the experience feel coherent even when the systems behind it were not.

Qualitative and quantitative input

Alongside usability testing, I worked with data scientists to A/B test key flows including login and signup nudging. Combining qualitative insight with behavioural data helped the team make more confident decisions across both day-to-day improvements and larger design changes.

Simplifying the tracking experience

Tracking was the most important and inherently complex part of the app. Users needed to understand what was happening with their delivery and what, if anything, they needed to do next — even when the underlying systems were inconsistent or fragmented.

The focus was on reducing cognitive load in delivery-related choices, clarifying statuses and next steps, and improving the logic of key journeys. In a context where reliability shapes trust, the experience needed to feel structured and predictable at every step.

Redesigned tracking experience — statuses, next steps, and delivery choices

Turning complex systems into clear UX

A significant part of the work involved translating operational and regulatory complexity into experiences that felt manageable. This meant working closely with domain experts to understand processes most designers rarely encounter — VAT and customs handling, BankID-based identity verification, address ownership, and multi-channel notification logic.

Each area required detailed system mapping before any design work could begin, followed by careful information architecture and interaction design across the many edge cases involved.

Account and identity flows, VAT and customs process mapping

Outcome

The app improved continuously over three years — through sustained optimisation and a redesign grounded in real product knowledge. Tracking became clearer, account and identity flows were simplified, and technically complex journeys were given coherent structure. Weekly usability testing became a core part of the product process, and collaboration between UX, engineering, and data grew significantly stronger.

The redesign was not an isolated event. It grew out of sustained product work, which helped create a more realistic and durable direction — one that PostNord's new in-house team could continue from a solid foundation.

Learnings

On redesign

Long-term product knowledge leads to better redesign decisions. Context built over time is hard to replicate in a short discovery phase.

On complexity

Complex systems don't need more features — they need more structure. Great UX is often about making constraints invisible, not removing them.
